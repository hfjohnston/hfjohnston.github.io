Research

  • Giant Planet Formation

    Giant planets contain the majority of mass and angular momentum within a system, meaning they dictate much of the planetary architecture. However, they are rare and only found around 1 in 5 stars at best.

  • Disc Evolution

    Understanding exactly how planet-forming discs evolve and change over in time in response to the host star(s) is key to understanding exactly how protoplanetary discs evolve into debris discs, and eventually (potentially habitable) planetary systems.

  • Host Stars

    The host star has strong influence over its natal protoplanetary disc. For example, intermediate-mass stars have the highest occurrence rate of giant planets. They evolve differently from their low-mass counterparts and this directly impacts the planet forming environment of the disk.

Career

I grew up in Dundee, Scotland, where I went to school and later completed my MSci in Physics at the University of Dundee in 2020. My master thesis, supervised by Dr Aurora Sicilia Aguilar, involved studying starspots in young, accreting stars by modelling their photospheric emission and comparing to the observations.

I then moved to Leeds, UK, in 2020 to start my PhD at the University of Leeds under the supervision of Dr Olja Panic. My thesis consisted of modelling giant planet formation around intermediate-mass stars. I was especially interested in how the host star properties can impact the disc dynamics and therefore influence planet formation outcomes.

In 2024, I moved to Exeter, UK, to work as a postdoctoral researcher at the University of Exeter, in Dr Seba Mairno's research group. I am currently working on the dispersal of protoplanetary discs into debris discs.
